The History of Rome


Rome was founded in 753 B.C. by Romulus and Remus, the sons of Mars, whom the romans dub as the "God of War". Both sons were left to drown in a basket on the Tiber river by a king near the ancient city of Alba Longa and were later rescued. The two sons lived their lives as warriors and defeated the same king that left them for dead. They then built their own city on the river’s banks in 753 B.C. Greed soon overcame the two brothers and after killing his brother, Romulus went on to become the first king of Rome, which interestingly enough is named for him. Kings from the earlier Italian civilisations followed Romulus in a non-hereditary succession to build an empire that was a force to be reckpned with. Over the years there have been seven legendary kings of Rome, namely; Romulus, Numa Pompilius, Tullus Hostilius, Ancus Martius, Lucius Tarquinius Priscus (Tarquin the Elder), Servius Tullius and Tarquinius Superbus, otherwise known as Tarquin the Proud (534-510 B.C.).


The Roman era ended in 509 B.C. when the seventh king, Tarquinius Superbus, whom ancient historians portrayed as cruel and tyrannical man, was overthrown. This overthrow was partly sparked by an uprising over the rape of a noblewoman, by Lucretia, the king’s son, whom unsurprisingly, was not punished. Whatever the cause of the uprising, Rome was turned from a monarchy into a republic, or “property of the people.”. It then came to be built on seven hills, known as “the seven hills of Rome”—Esquiline Hill, Palatine Hill, Aventine Hill, Capitoline Hill, Quirinal Hill, Viminal Hill and Caelian Hill.


Julius Caesar’s Rise To Power


When the victorious Pompey returned to Rome from battle, he formed an alliance known as the First Alliance with the wealthy Marcus Licinius Crassus (who suppressed a slave rebellion led by Spartacus in 71 B.C.) and a rising star in Roman politics: Julius Caesar. From his alliance with Pompey and Crassus, Caesar received the governorship of three wealthy provinces in Gaul beginning in 58 B.C.; he then set about conquering the rest of the region for Rome. After Pompey’s wife Julia (Caesar’s daughter) died in 54 B.C. and the death of Crassus in battle, the alliance was broken. With Roman politics in chaos, Pompey stepped in as sole consul in 53 B.C. Caesar’s military glory in Gaul and his increasing wealth had eclipsed Pompey’s, and the latter teamed with his Senate allies to undermine Caesar's leadership. In 49 B.C., Caesar and one of his men crossed the Rubicon, a river on the border between Italy from Cisalpine Gaul to invade Italy which ignited a civil war. In the aftermath, Caesar emerged as dictator of Rome for life in 45 B.C.


The Rise of Augustus


Less than a year later, Julius Caesar was murdered on March 15, 44 B.C., by a group of his enemies (which was led by republican nobles Marcus Brutus and Gaius Cassius). A Consul by the name of Mark Antony, Caesar’s great-nephew and adopted heir, Octavian, joined forces to crush Brutus and Cassius and divided power in Rome between themselves, with another party, ex-consul Lepidus, This was later known as the Second Alliance. With Octavian in the western provinces, Antony the east, and Lepidus Africa, tensions began to rise in 36 B.C. and the second alliance was dissolved. In 31 B.C., Octavian triumped over the forces of Antony and Queen Cleopatra of Egypt (who was rumored to be the lover of Julius Caesar). After recieving word of their heavy defeat, Antony and Queen Cleopatra committed suicide.
